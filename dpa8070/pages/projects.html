Project 2: Replicating a Scene

Oct 21st, 2020
For this project, you must recreate a picture as accurately as you can, creating the models, shaders, textures, and lighting. Please choose a picture of adequate difficulty. The picture should include some modeling tasks but not too many (a few marbles is too little, avoid vegetation or real persons), at least 4 interesting shaders/textures, and not just diffuse light. Please create the models, shaders/textures and lights, and render your scene. Render your scene from the perspective of the picture and from three different perspectives. The minimum pixel size is 800x600.

I wanted to pick a scene that was easy to model and allow me to focus on the lighting and shading. I chose not to create the pear because I didn't want to bite off more than I can chew. I hoped that by narrowing the scope of the project, I could deliver a more realistic image (rather than attempting to recreating everything and falling short of photorealism). I started by modeling all of the objects in the scene. The cutlery was a lot more challenging than I had expected. I manually unwrapped the plates, placemat, and tablecloths. When modeling the plates and placemat, I chose to leave out the detailed patterns and keep the mesh simple. I used automatic mapping for the cutlery because I planned to use procedural shading for those objects. I started creating lights and applying shaders. I found an HDRI to use for environment lighting. I played with its warmth in Photoshop to try and match the temperature in the reference image. I kept adding and deleting different lights and playing with their colors to try to match the image. I then went back to shading and prepared the texture maps wherever needed. For the plates, I painted a custom displacement map to create fancy patterns. To texture the placement, I found an image on Pottery Barn's online store that I liked. I used that for the diffuse color and painted a displacement map to match it. I downloaded cloth texture maps from Quixel. I played with standard Arnold shaders for the silverware and used fractal noise to add bum and roughness to it. The final scene has 4 lights (1 directional, 1 HDRI environment, and 2 Arnold area lights with a bluish tint).

Project 1: Snoopy Keychain

Sept 22nd, 2020
Problem: Modeling a Car or Animal based on an Object. For this assignment, create a model of your selected object using polygonal modeling, NURBS modeling, or a combination of the two. Make the final object appear as close to the real-life object as possible.

For this project, I decided to model a 3D model of one of my keychains of Snoopy and Woodstock lying down on a carved pumpkin. I felt that this would be quite a challenge, as it had 2 characters and an object. I have never done character modeling before, and I wanted to change that. I initially thought that modeling Snoopy would be the hardest of the three, but it turned out to be the pumpkin: although a symmetrical organic object, the carvings proved to be very hard to get right. I had to restart the pumpkin several times from scratch to understand how to approach it. For the final model, I created a normal pumpkin (grooves and all) and then proceeded to carve out the face on its front. I used standard modeling tools to create this project, like the Multi-Cut tool, the Insert Edge Loop tool, Mirror, Soft Selection, to name a few. Modeling Snoopy and Woodstock turned out to be easier than I had anticipated since the characters look more like gestural shapes even in the original keychain. I have always been bad at topology, so I wanted to do my best to improve. I improved as I went along. I can tell that the topology on the pumpkin is still improper, but there are no n-gons anywhere on any of the models. The topology on Snoopy seems to be fine everywhere but the arms and legs. I think Woodstock’s topology is fine, but I may need to remove reduce some of the unnecessary edge loops. Overall, I’m quite satisfied with this result.
