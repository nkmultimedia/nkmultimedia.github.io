Exercise 11

Problem: Watch an animated film and write about examles of three principles of animation. Share video clips or screenshots of the example in context.

I chose to watch the film The Thief and the Cobbler. Richard Williams is my favorite animator, and I personally marvel at the virtuosity in this film. Rather than point out 3 different principles of animation from various parts of the film, I thought it would be a fun exercise to breakdown and identify all 12 principles from a single scene. Here's the 20 second clip I chose to dissect:


  1. Squash and Stretch: The changes in his face.
  2. Anticipation: He flutters his hand before making the deck of cards appear.
  3. Staging: Look at this frame. Need I say more? The center-weighted composition makes you see what you need, and the lighting sets an ominous tone.
  4. Straight ahead action and pose to pose: The broad character movements definitely would’ve required pose to pose, whereas I feel like the card scattering at the end are an example of straight ahead action.
  5. Follow Through and Overlapping Action: The sleeves on his robes dangling around is an example of follow through. The timing on his hands (even when the movement seems symmetrical) are slightly offset from each other to create overlapping action.
  6. Slow In and low Out: So many examples to pick from; his hands changing direction is one. They slow down before reaching the extreme pose, and then speed up again in the opposite direction
  7. Arc: The movement of the cards as he shows off his skills. Arcs are everywhere in this clip, but this is the easiest to spot.
  8. Secondary action: His crazy facial expressions (there’s even a bit where his beard stretches out a little and curls back in, although that might be because his head is bobbing around, in which case that would be categorized as follow through)
  9. Timing: Accurate lip sync. His hands are mostly fluid, but stagger for emphasis wherever needed. The cards fall with air resistance. His robes flop around with a weight to them. The list is endless.
  10. Exaggeration: His eye ball is popping way out of it’s socket and looks plain crazy.
  11. Solid drawing: This clip was traditionally animated, and yet we can still see skillfully it was made. All movement feels believable. There's no twinning in any of the poses or movement. The character's anatomy is on point, except for when they are "deformed" to improve fluidity. We can see a foreshortening effect as he moves his hands closer and further away from us. Oh, and he has 6 fingers on each hand, plus an extra joint on each finger (bet you didn't catch that one!), but it's so skillfully animated that we don't percieve it. Solid drawing!
  12. Appeal: How can you not be intrigued by this character? He’s so spectacularly weird. He's got so many rings to show his lavishness, his facial hair and angular eyes let us know he's the villain, and his shoulders are hunched up like a vulture.

Exercise 10

Problem: Make your model from project 1 look as great as you can, with shading, lighting, and rendering, so it can be used for our class reel. Place it on a table or on a floor or in front of a background. Create a moving camera that shows your scene (your animation should be about 30-90 frames long). Render your scene.

I began the process by creating UV layouts for the pumpkin, Snoopy, and Woodstock. This allowed me to use Photoshop to paint color maps. I tried to replicate the grime and degradation from the actual object (It’s about 15 years old). I used 3 point lighting to light the scene. I used a directional light for the key light, a point light for fill, and an area light for the rim light. I played around with the lights individually to get decent lighting. I then proceeded to add bump maps to the surfaces. I used Maya mountain textures for Woodstock, and created my own bump maps for the other objects based on their color maps. For the base surface, I used another mountain texture for diffuse and bump maps. I used Nuke to run the image sequence and create the ping-pong playback.
Render settings:

  • Render format: Half-precision EXR
  • Render: Arnold
  • Samples:
    • Camera: 3
    • Diffuse: 3
    • Specular: 5
    • Transission: 2
    • SSS: 2
    • Volume Indirect: 2

Exercise 9: Shading and Lighting

Problem: Create two spheres: orange and jupiter. Create shaders and lighting to match the pictures as close as possible.

For both of the objects, I started out by applying an aiStandardShader, and creating 3 point lighting for the scene using a Maya directional light for the key light, and an Arnold area light for the fill and rim lights. For shading the orange, I set the base color and added Brownian noise as a bump map. For Jupiter, I started by creating a ramp node and adding various colors to represent different parts of the planet. I tried to add the vortex by using Maya’s leather texture and combining both in a layer node. I used this as the base color for Jupiter. I then used some wispy noise as a displacement map.

Exercise 7: NURBS Shoe

Problem: Finish modeling the given shoe using NURBS Tools

To model this shoe, I began by lofting Bezier curves to form the base of the shoe. I beveled the edge to of the top to make it more aesthetically pleasing. I used the extrude tool to form the straps, and the resolve tool to create the heel and the back of the shoe. I finally split my curves into halves and lofted them together to cap the open surfaces.

Exercise 6: Watch Tutorials

Problem: Watch 10 lessons of a tutorial series on a specific process in Maya and briefly describe what you learned

For this exercise, I found a course titled “ Learn Maya - Character Head Modeling for Beginners ” on Udemy taught by 3D Character Instructor Nalini Kanta Jena. The 1st two videos covered prepping and setting up reference images on image planes in Maya. The 3rd video went over the anatomy of eyeballs and demonstrated the approach to model them using a combination of 2 different deformed spheres. The 4th video covered the technique to create eyelids by using the Create Polygon Tool and extruding edges. The 5th video went over blocking the basic lip shapes using the same technique as eyelids. The 6th video illustrated modeling the cheek and connecting the mouth to the eyes. The 7th video discussed blocking out the forehead and overall shape of the skull. One useful technique demonstrated was enabling the Insert with Edge Flow option in the Insert Edge Loop tool. The 8th went over creating the neck by extruding and rotating edges to create a bust of sorts. The 9th video covered the anatomy and constructing of nostrils, completing the rough model. The 10th video started the next unit which deals with detailing. This video demonstrated how to detail the lips by using appropriately positioned edge loops to both add details and support the existing edges to harden them as required. My biggest take away from this series was the importance of understanding anatomy before beginning a model.

Exercise 5: Lamp

Problem: Create a model of the a lamp image using NURBS tools

We were given a single reference image of a lamp to model. I began the process by creating a bezier curve to draw out the cross section of the lamp. I used the revolve tool to turn this into a NURBS surface, and then went back to the original curve to refine the shape a bit. I repeated this same process for the lamp shade. For the base platform, I created multiplie NURBS squares of different sizes (each representing cross sections of the platform at points where the shape changes), and used the loft functions to build a surface.

Exercise 3: Lemon Squeezer

I started out by creating a cylinder to model the squeezer. I had start and restart a few times to figure out how to get the propper shape. I ended up extruding faces to create the indentation of the squeezer, and added edge loops to pull the sharp edges out. Exercise 2: Solar System

Problem: Create a solar system with a sun, planets and moons. Animated the planets and moons to rotate and orbit as required

For this assignment, I decided to stick to spheres (and a torus for Saturn's ring) and use textures to distinguish between the different planets and moons. I found texture maps for these objects from various sources online, and applied them as the color map on lambert shaders. I decided to avoid using manual keyframes for the animation. Instead, I felt that I could save a lot of time by using expressions to control the rotation. I had all the planets and moons grouped in a way that made it easy to add expressions appropriately. All the objects rotate and orbit at unique speeds.
